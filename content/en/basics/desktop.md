# The Desktop

When Wagashi Linux finishes starting up, the first thing you'll see is the desktop.

That's where everything begins.

Everything else starts from here.

---

## What you're looking at

The desktop is, essentially, a place where things happen.

Depending on which edition you're using, it might look a little different. Don't worry about that. The idea is the same everywhere.

You'll usually find a taskbar. It's often at the bottom of the screen. That's where you'll see your open applications and a few useful shortcuts.

You'll also find a menu. Open it and you'll find the programs installed on your system.

You may see a few icons on the desktop itself. Those are shortcuts to things you'll probably use often.

And then there's the rest.

The empty space.

You can fill it with windows, use it to organize your work, or simply leave it alone. It's your desktop. There isn't a right or wrong way to use it.

---

## Opening something

The easiest way to open an application is usually through the menu.

Click it and have a look around.

You'll find things like a file manager, a web browser, a text editor, and a terminal.

And one important thing.

If you don't know what something does, it's okay to open it anyway.

Curiosity tends to teach more than most manuals.

The worst thing that can happen is that you close the window a few seconds later.

---

## Moving things around

Windows can be moved, resized, minimized, and closed.

* Move: drag the top bar of the window.
* Resize: drag the edges or corners.
* Minimize: click the minimize button. The window will disappear from view, but it will still be running.
* Close: click the X. If there's unsaved work, you'll usually be warned first.

Don't be afraid to experiment.

Windows are meant to be moved around.

---

## The taskbar

The taskbar helps you keep track of what's open.

If you have several windows open and one seems to have mysteriously disappeared, look there first.

It's probably still open.

Click it and it'll come right back.

You'll usually find a clock, volume controls, and a few system options there as well.

They're there.

Have a poke around.

You'll find them quickly enough.

---

## Right-click

When in doubt, try right-clicking.

Seriously.

It solves more situations than it probably should.

Right-clicking on the desktop will usually give you options for changing the wallpaper, organizing icons, or opening a terminal.

Right-clicking on a file will show options for opening, copying, moving, or deleting it.

And right-clicking on a window will usually show options related to that window.

It doesn't break anything.

It just shows you more possibilities.

---

## One more thing

The desktop isn't the important part.

It's simply where things begin.

You don't need to understand everything you see today.

Start with what you need.

The rest will make sense when the time comes.

And if something feels unfamiliar, remember this:

Nobody was born knowing how to use Linux.

Or Windows.

Or any computer, for that matter.

We all started the same way.

By clicking on things and wondering what they did.

---

*Note: depending on which edition of Wagashi Linux you're using, your desktop may look different from what's described here. The ideas are the same, even if the layout changes.*
