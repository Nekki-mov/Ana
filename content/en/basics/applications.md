# Applications

An application is simply a program.

A web browser is an application.

A music player is an application.

A text editor is an application.

If it opens a window and helps you do something, it's probably an application.

---

## Where to find them

Most applications live in the menu.

Open it and you'll find everything installed on your system.

They're usually organized into categories such as Internet, Office, Graphics, Sound and Video, and more.

Take a moment to explore.

You might discover tools you didn't even know you already had.

---

## The ones you'll use most

Every Wagashi Linux installation comes with a collection of applications ready to use from the start.

At a minimum, you'll find:

* A file manager for browsing folders and files.
* A text editor for writing and editing plain text.
* A terminal for running commands. Don't worry about that one just yet. We'll get there.
* A web browser for accessing the internet.
* An image viewer for looking at photos and illustrations.

Some editions include even more tools.

The best way to discover them is simply to open them and see what they do.

---

## Installing more applications

Sooner or later, you'll need something that isn't already installed.

That's perfectly normal.

That's what dango is for.

dango is Wagashi Linux's way of finding, installing, and managing applications.

There's an entire chapter dedicated to it, and you'll get there when the time comes.

For now, there's only one thing you need to remember:

Installing software on Linux is usually much easier than people expect.

---

## Removing applications

Installed something and changed your mind?

You can remove it.

That's it.

Nothing is stuck forever.

Nothing is permanent.

It's your computer.

Arrange it in whatever way makes sense to you.

---

## About software you find online

Sometimes you'll come across software that's distributed as a file you download.

Installers.

Packages.

Compressed archives.

And other things with increasingly creative names.

While many of them work perfectly fine, the recommended way to install software on Wagashi Linux is through dango.

It's not a rule.

It's simply safer, easier, and usually a lot less confusing.

If you're not sure what to do, start with dango.

---

## One more thing

You don't need to know exactly what an application does before opening it.

Explore it.

See what options it offers.

Try things.

And if it isn't what you were looking for, just close it.

Most people discover their favorite tools in exactly that way.

Through curiosity.

Not obligation.
